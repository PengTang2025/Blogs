Last year, when I was off work and re-evaluate my career path, I found myself reflecting on the rapid development of Large Language
Models (LLMs). At the time, they were hailed as the **ultimate solution** for Artificial Intelligence—a claim I found deeply debatable
. Here are my thoughts and discussions during that time.  

## The Tape Recorder and the Pigeon

In Chinese internet culture, the *essence of humanity* is described as **a Tape Recorder** (imitation and repetition) at some time, 
and **a Pigeon** (no-shows and delays) at the other time. I believe it would be hard for people to deny that. This presents a 
fascinating paradox: We want LLMs to become as same as us, yet no one truly wants an assistant that inherits the repetitive nature 
of a Tape Recorder or the tardiness of a Pigeon. This leads to a structural contradiction: the more we train AI to resemble humans, 
the more it adopts our inherent limitations. Conversely, once an AI is purged of these human *weaknesses*, it fails to meet our 
expectations of being *human-like*.  

The current LLM, performs excellently as a tape recorder. Many people have observed that the depth and breadth of its responses are 
strongly correlated with the question itself; furthermore, the questioner’s level of expertise can influence the LLM’s capabilities.
For example, when a biological researcher discusses how to perform data analysis, the LLM may forget that it is a top-tier algorithm
expert, or even forget basic algorithms like dynamic programming. (Later, there might be a full discussion stemming from a 
question like, “Are the random numbers generated by aimless.js more ‘random’ than those generated by random()?”)  

## Are LLMs the future of AI?
Many in the business world want LLMs to be all-rounders instead of text-processing models, despite their current struggles with 
formal logic and mathematics. But as a researcher exactly in the field, I tend to be a bit pessimistic. Because mathematics
demands absolute precision, whereas emotional understanding requires intuition and the *uncertainty* of probabilistic neural 
networks. We can build precise algorithms and empathetic LLMs, but we cannot achieve **a perfect LLM**. Because this is the central 
tension between *precision and ambiguity* in the current trajectory of AI development.  

> Regarding this matter, ChatGPT itself remarked: “It’s like expecting a person to possess both Gauss’s rationality and Dostoevsky’
s depth of feeling—to be both a flawless engineer and an emotionally stable poet.”
  
However, as a researcher in the field, I’m optimistic at the same time. No one is perfect, but we can have specialists against 
the generalists. We can have an emotionally intelligent partner that can call upon others as needed—whether they’re rigorous 
mathematical algorithms or expert linguists.  
(After a year, now we meet Multi-Agent Workflows, moreover, Human-in-the-loop.)  

## LLMs and the Boundaries of Responsibility  

I suggest that professionals in the field should act more as **Chinese-style parents**. While outsiders see how amazing, capable, 
and academically accomplished the child is, a Chinese-style parent would say, “No, no, there are still issues here and there; 
they’re not that great at all.” The reason these professionals are considered *insiders* isn’t because they can praise things 
more eloquently than outsiders based on their expertise, but they can immediately spot problems and limitations.  

For this reason, I do not believe that an indiscriminate wave of unemployment will occur. Many people are anxious about that and 
believe everyone will eventually lose their jobs, including those currently considered *secure*, such as teachers, doctors, and 
programmers. However, I believe that aside from repetitive, mechanical tasks (which are actually being replaced by standardized 
procedures rather than AI), other industries must retain their human workforce. The most important reason for this is that **AI 
cannot assume responsibility**. Humans are not machines. We make mistakes. But what compels us to perform each task flawlessly is 
our awareness of responsibilities and the consequences of failing. AI, however, cannot shoulder this responsibility. If we 
force *punishment* into its training, it will simply go on strike, providing only basic, conservative outputs while losing its 
flexibility and creativity. But without a mechanism for accountability, how can AI guarantee it won’t make mistakes? It’s like 
the joke that **accountants can’t be replaced because AI can’t go to jail in their place**.   

## Extensions from 2026 
It is true that the development of AI will lead to job losses. However, I maintain the view that rising productivityis driving higher 
skill requirements for workers, rather than indiscriminate replacement. In 2026, we notice that job postings for non-premium
translators, graphic designers, and programmers now all require proficiency in AI tools. At the same time, we’ve also seen the 
introduction of regulations and insurance related to digital workers. The weight of societal change can feel like a mountain bearing down on individuals. Structural pressures like these rarely resolve without deliberate policy intervention. 

I’ve always believed that AI development should **first focus on** providing essential support, which means helping those in need, 
rather than merely adding frills. And it should *never* take jobs away from ordinary people. For example, in my vision, autonomous
driving should first assist the disabled in getting around and support long-haul truck drivers, before being developed into 
driverless taxis. AI-generated art should be applied first to the repetitive and mentally taxing task of creating commercial 
schematics, rather than book illustrations. However, this year, the first to lose their jobs were backend engineers (my former 
position), and here I am still worrying about others, ha-ha.
